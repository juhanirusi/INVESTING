# Balance Sheet Analysis

Try to calculate these ratios on both the trailing twelve months level as well as latest quarter level!


## Cash To Current Assets

To calculate the cash to current assets ratio:

⚠️ Cash & Cash Equivalents / Current Assets * 100

A ratio under 40% or less is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a ratio that is higher than 60% or more!


## Inventory To Current Assets

To calculate the inventory to current assets ratio:

⚠️ Inventories / Current Assets * 100

A ratio under 40% or less is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a ratio that is higher than 60% or more!


## Receivable To Current Assets

To calculate the receivable to current assets ratio:

⚠️ Accounts Receivable / Current Assets * 100

A ratio under 40% or less is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a ratio that is higher than 60% or more!


## Payable To Current Assets

To calculate the payable to current assets ratio:

⚠️ Accounts Payable / Current Assets * 100

A ratio between 20% to 40% is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a ratio that is higher than 60% or more!


## Interest Coverage Ratio

To calculate the interest coverage ratio:

⚠️ EBIT / Interest Expenses

EBIT = Earnings Before Interest & Taxes

A ratio higher than 15 is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a ratio that is higher than 60% or more!


## Working Capital To Debt Ratio

To calculate the working capital to debt ratio:

⚠️ Working Capital / (Short Term Debt + Long Term Debt)

Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

A WC to debt ratio higher than 0.20 is considered GOOD!

Try to avoid companies with a negative WC to debt ratio!


## Free Cash Flow VS Long-term Debt

To calculate if the business can pay of it’s debts within three years, which is one of our investment criterias:

⚠️ 3 x Free Cash Flow > Long-term Debt


## Financial Leverage Ratio

To calculate a company’s financial leverage ratio:

⚠️ Total Debt / Total Equity

In general, a ratio near 1 is preferable!

An extremely low ratio may indicate that the company’s operating margins are so tight that it is unwilling or unable to borrow!
